At work, we're currently undertaking a big AI training exercise, from the basics of LLMs, to agentic development, fine-tuning, and spec driven development. Since I love a food-based analogy, I thought I'd try my hand at applying a cooking analogy to AI in software development.

## Make me a Full English Breakfast

Let's imagine you are asked by your client to make a Full English Breakfast. You probably have some concept of this, what elements are involved, how to make it, and you might be able to do a pretty good job. Maybe your view of the Full English is similar to your client's, but probably there are nuances.

### Vibe Coding, aka "The Home Improviser"

There's another problem – you're a complete kitchen newbie. You've never been in a kitchen, you don't know what ingredients you have, and you only have the very general concepts of cooking. Still, you are plucky, and you try your best to bash out a Full English, with the knowledge you have, tools and ingredients at your disposal.

Will the kitchen end up a mess? Maybe.  
Could you repeat it the same, next time? Possibly?
Will it meet the client's expectations? Uncertain.

So what's wrong with home improvised cooking? Nothing! If it's just for you, or if you're testing something out - a technique, an ingredient, a recipe - then home improvisation can be perfect.

On the other hand, an improvised meal can end up tasting like slop, if you're not careful. If you care about creating a consistent meal, creating something of high quality, or especially if you're serving or selling the food to others, then you might need to up your game.

### Context Priming (like Agents.md), aka "A basic kitchen walkthrough"

Now imagine that just as you're asked to make the Full English, the client gives you a bit more knowledge, downloaded into your brain. You now have:

- All the basic information about how this kitchen operates,
- A list of general cooking best practices, that relate to making breakfasts (how to avoid burning toast, what order things should be cooked in),
- A good understanding of how to clean as you go,
- A process to follow if you need help with a step,
- A basic quality control method

This information is going to disappear from your brain as soon as you're done cooking, or maybe before, if you take too long, but you are much more likely to make a Full English that your client will enjoy!

### Spec Driven Development, AKA "the recipe process"

This time, imagine that before you're allowed to start, your client makes you go through a recipe creation process. In the process, you define:

- A set of criteria that the meal should meet,
- Your client's taste preferences,
- The ingredients list, maybe even down to the brands to use,
- Very detailed recipes for each component of the dish,
- A quality control process to ensure that each component tastes right,
- A step-by-step breakdown, with mini recipes to follow,
- A process to keep your client up-to-date on the progress

And since you're creating these specifications with your client before you're even allowed into the kitchen, you're probably more likely to succeed – especially if:

- your client is specific about what they want, and/or
- if your client is a good cook, and/or
- your client reviews your recipe with you and provides feedback

### MCP, AKA "the high-tech tools"

For this specific Full English Breakfast, your client wants deep-fried hash browns and 63º eggs. You might be able to jury-rig a way to make these with a basic kitchen setup, but luckily your client has provided you with a deep fryer, a sous-vide cooker, and the instructions for how to use them! So generous!

You probably don't need these fancy pieces of cooking equipment, and they aren't going to be useful for everything, but when you find the right use case, they will elevate the meal!

### Skills, aka "the station specialist"

Now it seems like your client has gotten serious about their Full English, because they've hired an assistant to help you out! Now, as soon as you hit the step in your recipe that mentions beans, or if you go to the pantry to grab the beans, or if someone nearby mentions beans, Belinda is going to appear, and she's going to take over. Belinda knows all about baked beans, from canned beans to home made, from pressure cooked to fire-roasted. She's got you covered, so just hand over the job.

This also means that you don't need to try to keep track of all the beans-related information in your recipe, because your skilled assistant comes with it built in.

### RAG, aka "the kitchen's library of recipes and customer preferences"

In this kitchen, you churn out a lot of Full English Breakfasts every day, so it would be pretty inconsistent to improvise every time, and it might not be very consistent even if you went through the recipe creation process for every plate - you could end up with different recipes that meet the same success criteria.

So because you're basically a Full English specialty kitchen now, your client has invested in a library dedicated to all the components of the dish. Instead of making up recipes every time, just go to the library, pull out the book for "Black Pudding", and away you go. It even has a section for how Mr Cortez and Ms Singh like their mushrooms seasoned!

### Fine tuning, aka "the culinary school apprenticeship"

You've decided that you are done with doing anything else in your life, and you just want to make breakfast for the rest of your days. And your client is happy about this, because this means all the time they spent instructing you on basic cooking skills can now be spent on the finer details about how THEY like their fried tomatoes.
